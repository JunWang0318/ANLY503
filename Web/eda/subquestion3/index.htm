Sub-Question 3: Is there a relationship between the cancer rate and the economic activities?


Since we are interested about the relationship between the cancer rate and the geographic location, we use tableau to create a treemap with the average cancer mortality rate from 2005 to 2015 for each states across united states.


Figure 16 (Tableau)


For the treemap above, darker colors represent higher average cancer mortality rate. As we can see, the top five states are Kentucky, Mississippi, West Virginia, Louisiana, and Arkansas. In addition, the least five states are Utah, Colorado, New Mexico, Arizona, and California. So now, we want to only focus on these 10 states and take a look at the economics activities for each states to investigate the relationship.


For each states, we plotted the pie chart for its average GDP output from 2005 to 2015. In general, among the total 11 categories, the portions for Finance and Manufacturing vary the most compare to the rest of the categories.

Figure 17 (ggplot)

As we can see, in general, the portions of manufacturing GDP for the top five states are higher than the least five states. Therefore, economic activities might be a good point to explore more in the study.


Here are the mortality race for the above 10 states, as we can see, in general, it is decreasing since 1999.



Figure 18 (ggplot)


We also plotted the incidence rate for the 10 states, and found that the incidence rate is increasing for Arkansas which is different from the general decreasing trending.



Figure 19 (ggplot)


So we pulled out the economic data for California and Arkansas to create the below bubble charts.



Figure 20 (ggplot)


As we can see, along with the decreasing cancer incidence rate, both the Manufacturing and Finance Real Estate number is decreasing for California since 2007. On the other hand, the trending for the Manufacturing and Finance Real Estate for Arkansas are also similar to the trending for the cancer incidence rate. For all the rates (Manufacturing, Finance, and Incidence), there is a drop from 2006 to 2011 and a big increase from 2011 to 2015. Therefore, we might be able to conclude that there is a relationship between the manufacturing GDP and cancer incidence rate.
