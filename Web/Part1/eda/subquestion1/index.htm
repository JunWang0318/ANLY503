Sub-Question 1: What is the overall trending for the the age-adjusted cancer incidence rate, mortality rate, and death rate?


Even though the incidence rate is increasing since 1999, it does not have a meaningful indication because its computation does not exclude the age effects. For example, a county having a higher percentage of elderly people may have a higher rate of death or hospitalization than a county with a younger population, merely because the elderly are more likely to die or be hospitalized. In order to make fairer comparisons between groups with different age distributions, we introduce the variable called Age adjusted rate. It can make the different groups more comparable, and same as before, the unit for the number is per 100,000 people. For future reference, all the rates we mention in our study are age-adjusted.



Figure 11 (Matplotlib)



Figure 12 (Matplotlib)


In above two charts, the blue bars represent the counts for cancer incidences and mortalities for each year across the United States, while the red line is the age-adjusted rate for the incidences and mortalities for each year. We can see that even though the total number of counts for both incidence and mortality are increasing since 1999, the actual age-adjusted rates are decreasing. This might due to the increase in elderly people who have cancers. When there are more elderly people within the country, the number of cancer count would increase, but the age-adjusted rate would decrease since it put less weight on the elderly people.


Then we decided to introduce a new variable feature--death rate which is computed by the number of mortalities divides the number of incidences for each year.



Figure 13 (Matplotlib/Pandas)


From the line chart above, we can see that there is a significant drop for the death rate since 1999, then there a fluctuation since 2009 and stabilized after 2013.
