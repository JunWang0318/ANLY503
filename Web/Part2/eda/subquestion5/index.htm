Sub-Question 5: Could chemical use correlate with cancers?


To determine if cancer is correlated with chemical use, it is better to divide the total number to each state, since the range of chemical use among all states is huge. After separating the total number to each state, we can see the following:

Figure 17 (Python Bokeh)


While more than half of the states do not have significant change, we can still see that the number of incidence in the rest of the states have increased. This comes to our question: are they correlate with their own chemical use?


To see the correlation between chemical use and cancer incidence rate, we use the following bubble chart to explore the correlation:


Figure 18 (Python Bokeh)


The size of each bubble indicates the discrepancy between chemical use and cancer rate: each bubble indicates one state. If the rank of chemical use among all states is significantly different than the rank if cancer rate, then the size of the bubble will be larger than those states which have similar chemical use rank and cancer rate rank in the U.S. (e.g. if the chemical use rank of certain states is 40 and its cancer rate rank among all states is also 40, then the bubble size should be really small; otherwise if one rank is 1 and another is 50 then the bubble size will be large).


According to the bubble chart above, we can see that among 50 states, there are 8 states that have a comparatively large discrepancy between chemical use and cancer rate. However, 84% of states show a positive correlation between cancer and chemical use. As shown in the figure above, we can see that those 42 states are located at the middle; if there is a straight line with a slope of 1, then these bubbles should be around that straight line.
