Sub-Question 5: Could chemical use correlate with cancers?


Applying chemical use on agricultural land could negatively affect the local environment. In this case, we want to explore if there is a relationship between chemical use and cancer incidence.


We first compared the trend of cancer incidence number and chemical use value from 2007 to 2012. From the line chart below, we can see that both values are increasing. Even though we cannot conclude that the relationship exists, it is worth to further explore their relationship later.



Figure 24 (Matplotlib)


Below is a paired categorical plot for chemical use, the y-axis is the chemical used value. In the beginning, the value used is slightly decreased between 1997 and 2002, however, later on, the value keeps increasing until 2012. The maximum average value is reached in 2012 which is nearly 16,000. From each year, we can also find that the range from all states in the United States is widely spread. In other words, the chemical used on agricultural land is significantly different state by state.


Figure 25 (Seaborn)


To further extract the information hidden behind use range, we plotted a violin chart. Among all categories under chemical, according to the boxplot shown below, the largest one is fungicide. Based on the violin char below, we can see that the majority of states have chemical use under 5000 units, which means that most of them are similar; However, we can observe that part of states have extremely high usage of chemical, some of them are even 4 times more than the average case. It is interesting to see if these outliers would have any effect on cancer.
